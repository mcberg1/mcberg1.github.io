Custom HPA Airsoft Gun

Designed from the ground up, a sleek airsoft gun that runs on compressed air.

For my 18th birthday, my parents got me an airsoft gun, since airsoft had been a hobby I wanted to start. However, less than 24 hours after I first went to the field with it, I had to know how it worked. This is where all of my airsoft adventures began.
This project specifically began with the "HPA engine". This is the common name among airsofters for the main internal assembly that is responsible for reciprocating, cycling ammunition, and controlling airflow down the barrel. This would be the equivalent of the bolt assembly on a real firearm.

Eventually, I began to want to make a usable clock from these tubes to have in my room. I started a new design to be laser cut in March of 2021. I wanted it to contain an old telephone bell I had, as an alarm, which explains the size of the case. I stained the case with a dark color stain, but quickly realized I was unhappy with how the whole thing looked, and the tubes went back to collecting dust.

Once the summer began during covid, however, the spark reignited, and I began this design for the clock. I started with the idea of having a small, modern, wooden box. Ideally, I would use capacitive touch to interface, and keep the outside clean. This is where my prototyping begins.

I used an arduino mega for easy prototyping of the capacitive touch interface, where I was testing the ability to sense through â…› inch plywood. This, as expected, required an increased sensitivity. I was concerned about sensor overlap, where two pads nearby each other would struggle to differentiate between which one should be active, and I hoped to be able to place them closer to the surface. I was also contemplating how I would mount the conductive pads in the correct position. I though about using the laser cutter for three dimensional operations, with careful raster engraving. Prototyping this showed that I was able to reliably create recesses with this process, in order to be able to mount the pads. Looking back, this could have been done on our CNC mill, though I was not yet experienced with the mill, and I liked the idea of doing it all on the laser cutter.

With this figured out, I started a model of the case in SOLIDWORKS. I began with the top plate, as it would contain the most features. Firstly, I created holes for the tubes to stick through. Next was the buttons, which was simply text to be engraved on the top. For indication of a button press, I decided to put very small holes above each button for an adressable RGB LED to shine through. I put recessed pockets on the underside of the top plate, directly behind the text buttons, for the capactive pads to mount. The rest of the walls were simply rectangles, sized to fit an arduino uno, the driver shield, and an ESP32 inside.
Ideally, I wanted to hide the edge grain of the plywood, and my solution was to miter each plate at 45 degrees, so they'd match up, hiding the edge grain. I did this in the laser cutter, using a jig to hold the parts at the correct angle.

Internally, the clock contains an ESP32, and an Arduino Uno, as mentioned before. The Arduino Uno is soley used as a driver for the display tubes. I did not use the ESP32 alone, to minimize extra wiring to the shield. The ESP was used to process the capacitive sensors, drive the LEDs, and to get the time from an NTP server, removing the need for a real time clock (RTC) module.

The top plate was iterated a few times before final assembly, to ensure that the capactive touch interface worked correctly. During this time, I also wrote the code for serial communication between the two controllers. I also implemented the NTP server integration on the ESP32. Eventually, the clock was working correctly outside of its case, and it was ready to be fully assembled. The top plate was glued to the sides, leaving only the bottom unattached. I planned to use miniature push pins to anchor the bottom plate, by passing them through the sides, and into the edge grain. This worked well, though made assembly slightly more difficult.

With the electronics all inside, the rest of the work was programming. Features included are an auto shut off timer, to preserve tube life, and five individual alarm times, which can be set and activated using the buttons (I planned to eventually link this to the bell as a separate module).
